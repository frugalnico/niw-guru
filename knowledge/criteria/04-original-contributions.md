# Criterion 4: Original Contributions of Major Significance

## Legal Standard
Evidence of the beneficiary's original scientific, scholarly, or business-related contributions of major significance in the field. Two sub-elements: (1) original — novel, not replication, (2) major significance — meaningful impact in the field, not just existence.

This is often the hardest criterion because "major significance" is subjective.

## Evidence Hierarchy (strongest to weakest)
1. Patent granted with evidence of industry adoption or licensing
2. Expert testimonial letters from independent experts (not co-founders/colleagues) describing impact on the field
3. Quantified adoption metrics — user count, downloads, revenue, companies using the technology
4. VC funding received as a result of the contribution (with amounts and firm names)
5. Third-party reviews/recognition — awards, app store ratings, industry recognition
6. Media coverage of the contribution with circulation figures
7. Product documentation — pitch deck, architecture, code (proves originality, not significance)

## Proving "Major Significance"

Adjudicators need impact BEYOND the beneficiary's own company:

| Evidence Type | Why It Works |
|--------------|-------------|
| Other companies/researchers using the contribution | Field-wide impact |
| Citations in academic papers | Scholarly recognition |
| Revenue/users at scale | Commercial significance |
| Industry awards for the contribution | Third-party validation |
| VC funding at significant valuation | Market validation |
| Expert letters from people OUTSIDE the org | Independent validation |
| Media coverage describing the contribution as innovative | Third-party characterization |

## Best Practices
- Expert letters are treated as ADVISORY OPINIONS, not proof — always corroborate with independent documentary evidence (patents, adoption data, citations, licensing agreements, press)
- Letters from co-founders or close colleagues are discounted as self-serving — get letters from independent experts with no business relationship
- Simply having a patent is NOT enough — show the patent has been implemented, licensed, or adopted by others
- Prove the work is original (not building on others' work) and that the beneficiary personally deserves credit even if there are co-contributors
- Include evidence of contracts, licenses, or agreements for use of the original work
- Cite exact user count, download count, revenue with dates — "improved performance" is insufficient; "doubled SLA performance to 99.9%" works
- Citations alone don't prove major significance without context about who is citing and why
- Assert significance through evidence, not adjectives — let metrics, expert quotes, and awards speak
- If the contribution was collaborative, submit evidence showing the beneficiary's specific role and that they should be credited
