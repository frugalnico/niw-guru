# Criterion 5: Authorship of Scholarly Articles

## Legal Standard
Evidence of the beneficiary's authorship of scholarly articles in the field, in professional or major trade publications or other major media.

Key distinction from Criterion 3: Criterion 3 = material written ABOUT the beneficiary by others. Criterion 5 = articles written BY the beneficiary. Both can be used in the same petition.

## Evidence Hierarchy (strongest to weakest)
1. Peer-reviewed journal article with DOI, impact factor, and citation count
2. Article in a major professional publication (e.g., IEEE Spectrum, Harvard Business Review) with editorial process
3. Conference proceedings (peer-reviewed, published by ACM/IEEE/etc.)
4. arXiv / preprint server papers (not peer-reviewed, but shows scholarly activity)
5. Contributor-platform articles (Forbes Council, LinkedIn) — published in major media but lower editorial bar
6. Blog posts (generally not accepted)

## Required Facts Per Article

| Fact | Source |
|------|--------|
| Full citation (authors, title, journal, year, DOI) | The article |
| Journal impact factor | Journal Citation Reports, Scimago |
| Whether peer-reviewed | Journal website "About" page |
| Citation count | Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar |
| Journal circulation/readership | SimilarWeb, journal press kit |
| Co-authors and affiliations | The article |

## Best Practices
- Always include journal ranking and citation count from Google Scholar
- For contributor-platform articles (Forbes Council), acknowledge the lower editorial bar but argue the vetting process and include reach metrics
- If the beneficiary is a minor co-author, specify their contribution; first or corresponding author status strengthens the claim
- The article topic must relate to the stated field of endeavor — explain the connection if not obvious
- Include the full published article as an exhibit, not just a citation
