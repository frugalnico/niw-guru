# O-1A & EB-1A: Extraordinary Ability

Same "extraordinary ability" standard, nearly identical criteria. This knowledge base covers both.

## Key Differences

| | O-1A | EB-1A |
|---|---|---|
| Visa type | Nonimmigrant (temporary) | Immigrant (green card) |
| Form | I-129 | I-140 |
| Criteria needed | At least 3 of 8 | At least 3 of 10 (same 8 + commercial success + display of work) |
| Additional | Advisory opinion letter required | Must show intent to continue work + substantially benefit U.S. |
| Scrutiny | Generally more lenient | Stricter — permanent benefit |

## Standard
"A level of expertise indicating that the individual is one of that small percentage who has risen to the very top of the field of endeavor."

## Two-Step Analysis (Kazarian)
1. **Step 1:** Does the evidence meet at least 3 criteria on plain language?
2. **Step 2:** Does the totality show sustained national/international acclaim?

Meeting 3 at Step 1 does NOT guarantee approval. Step 2 is where most denials happen.

## EB-1A Additional Requirements
- **Continue to work in the field** — submit employment contracts, offer letters, or statement of plans
- **Substantially benefit the U.S.** — letters explaining how the work benefits the U.S. on a national level

## How Adjudicators Read Petitions
- They skim 200+ pages — the support letter must be self-contained and clear
- They look for the 3 easiest criteria first
- Third-party evidence outweighs self-serving evidence
- Letters of support are advisory opinions, not proof — must be corroborated with independent documentary evidence
- 3 strong criteria > 6 weak ones
- Define the field narrowly so the beneficiary stands out

## Best Practices
- Every factual claim backed by documentary evidence, not just expert letters
- Each expert letter should address different criteria or aspects — no duplication
- Replace vague adjectives with sourced numbers
- Always include a totality/Step 2 argument at the end
