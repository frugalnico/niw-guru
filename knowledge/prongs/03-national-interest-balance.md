# Prong 3: Balance of National Interest

## Legal Standard
On balance, it would be beneficial to the U.S. to waive the job offer and labor certification requirements.

## Argument Structure

### 1. Why the job offer requirement doesn't apply
Adapt to the beneficiary's situation:
- **Entrepreneurs:** The beneficiary founded/co-founded the company — the employer-employee relationship is circular
- **Researchers:** The research agenda is driven by the individual's expertise, not a job description; the work transcends any single employer
- **Physicians in underserved areas:** Requiring labor certification would delay care to a population already underserved
- **Self-employed / consultants:** The nature of the work spans multiple clients or projects

### 2. Why labor certification would be impractical
- The beneficiary's unique combination of skills, knowledge, and track record is not readily testable through the PERM process
- The endeavor requires this specific individual's vision, expertise, or leadership
- Delaying through the labor certification process would harm the national interest by interrupting critical work

### 3. The benefit outweighs the cost
- The beneficiary's continued work directly addresses [the national problem from Prong 1]
- Interrupting the work would harm [specific population, research program, infrastructure project, business]
- No U.S. worker is displaced — the role requires the beneficiary's unique qualifications
- The work creates U.S. jobs, trains U.S. workers, generates economic value, or advances U.S. competitiveness (whichever apply)

### 4. Field-specific arguments
- **Researchers:** Ongoing experiments, longitudinal studies, or grant-funded projects would be disrupted; the U.S. benefits from retaining trained researchers
- **Physicians:** Patients in underserved areas would lose access to care; provider shortage data supports the waiver
- **Engineers:** Critical infrastructure projects depend on this individual's expertise; delays have public safety implications
- **Entrepreneurs:** The company employs U.S. workers and serves U.S. customers; requiring a job offer to oneself is structurally absurd
- **National security/defense:** The work supports U.S. strategic interests; requiring labor certification could expose sensitive projects

## Best Practices
- This prong is typically the shortest — 2-3 paragraphs
- Don't introduce new facts — synthesize what was established in Prongs 1 and 2
- Always tie back to the national importance from Prong 1
- Frame as: the U.S. benefits from this person doing this work; requiring a job offer would impede that benefit
- If the work creates U.S. jobs, mention it — directly addresses the labor certification concern
- For physicians: the HPSA/MUA argument is often decisive by itself
- For researchers with active grants: the grant is effectively a job offer from the U.S. government — argue this
- For entrepreneurs: the "I can't get a job offer from myself" argument is well-established but still state it explicitly
