# Reusable Argument Patterns

Argument structures that work across criteria and petition types.

## Pattern 1: The Layered Evidence Stack

**When to use:** Any criterion where you need to prove significance or prestige.

**Structure:**
1. State the claim
2. Cite the primary document exhibit
3. Cite an independent third-party source (press, award, expert letter)
4. Cite a quantitative metric
5. Cross-reference evidence from other criteria

**Example:**
> "The Beneficiary's membership in [Professional Council] constitutes membership in an association requiring outstanding achievements, as judged by experts in the field. [Professional Council] maintains an acceptance rate of approximately [X]% (See Exhibit 20), with over [N] members across [N]+ countries (Web Source 3). [Organization Contact], [Title], confirmed in a letter to USCIS that the Beneficiary was invited based on outstanding accomplishments in [area] (See Exhibit 20). The Beneficiary's acceptance was further enabled by the recognition garnered through venture capital funding from [top-tier VC firm] (See Exhibits 5-6) and the widespread adoption of [the Beneficiary's product] (See Exhibit 37)."

**Why it works:** Four different types of evidence (org letter, web data, VC backing, product metrics) all reinforcing the same point. An adjudicator would have to reject all four to deny the criterion.

## Pattern 2: The Cross-Reference Web

**When to use:** When the same evidence supports multiple criteria (very common in tech entrepreneur petitions).

**Structure:** In each criterion section, explicitly reference exhibits from other criteria:

> "As further evidenced by the Beneficiary's membership to distinguished organizations such as the On Deck Fellowship, Beta Fellowship, and A16z Speedrun (See Exhibits 12-32), the venture capital awards received by the Beneficiary demonstrate recognition by multiple independent expert bodies."

**Why it works:** Makes the petition feel like a cohesive narrative rather than 8 separate arguments. Also means each criterion is supported by more evidence than what's directly listed under it.

## Pattern 3: The "Not Just X" Framing

**When to use:** When an adjudicator might reduce the evidence to something ordinary.

**Structure:** Preempt the dismissal by showing what makes this different from the ordinary version.

**Example (membership):**
> "The Forbes Business Council is not merely a professional networking group; it is an invitation-only community with a 10-11% acceptance rate, requiring applicants to hold a senior executive position at a company with at least $500,000 in revenue or financing, and further requiring that applicants demonstrate tangible outstanding accomplishments as reviewed by a selection committee of experts."

**Example (VC as award):**
> "Venture capital funding is not merely a business transaction; it is a competitive, merit-based selection process in which [Firm] evaluated [N] applicants and selected only [M], based on the entrepreneur's demonstrated excellence in building technology companies."

## Pattern 4: The Quantitative Anchor

**When to use:** Every single paragraph. Always.

**Rule:** Never write a paragraph without at least one specific number.

| Instead of | Write |
|------------|-------|
| "a large hackathon" | "a hackathon with 1,696 participants and a 4.6% acceptance rate" |
| "a prestigious VC firm" | "a VC firm managing $35B+ in assets with 1,000+ portfolio companies" |
| "widespread media coverage" | "coverage in 6 outlets with a combined circulation of 16.8M unique visits" |
| "a selective fellowship" | "a fellowship that reviewed thousands of applications to select 150 fellows" |

## Pattern 5: The Totality Argument (Step 2)

**When to use:** After all criteria are argued, as a closing section.

**Structure:** Summarize the full picture in 2-3 paragraphs showing sustained acclaim:

1. **Timeline narrative:** "From [year] to [year], the Beneficiary has [built/published/led/judged]..."
2. **Breadth of recognition:** "The Beneficiary's work has been recognized by [VC firms], [media outlets], [award organizations], [university hackathons], and [professional associations] across [N] countries."
3. **Field positioning:** "Taken together, this evidence demonstrates that the Beneficiary has risen to the very top of the field of [Field], as required by the O-1A standard."

## Anti-Patterns (What NOT to Do)

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Fix |
|-------------|-------------|-----|
| "The Beneficiary is extraordinary" | Conclusory — the adjudicator decides this, not the attorney | Let the evidence speak; cite numbers and third-party validation |
| Identical paragraphs for each membership | Adjudicator notices the copy-paste, loses trust | Customize each paragraph with specific facts about that organization |
| Adjective soup ("renowned, prestigious, esteemed, distinguished") | Empty filler that signals weak evidence | Replace every adjective with a sourced fact |
| Burying the strongest evidence | Adjudicator may stop reading | Lead with the strongest criterion, strongest event, strongest evidence |
| Including weak evidence "for completeness" | Dilutes the strong evidence | Omit anything that doesn't pass the "would this impress the adjudicator?" test |
